# hook-writing

## when to use

Use when a user asks for a hook, an opening line, a headline, or says an existing post "needs a stronger opener." This skill produces hook candidates that other writing skills (x-thread, linkedin-post, discord-announcement, telegram-broadcast) drop into their own structures.

## workflow

1. Read `.atelier/memory/voice.md` if present. A hook that's technically strong but off-voice will still get cut in review — check it before generating options.
2. Check `.atelier/memory/instincts.md` for overrides (banned topics, claims that need sourcing).
3. Confirm the mobile-fold constraint for the target platform: assume the reader decides whether to keep reading within roughly the first 210 characters, and on X, within the first visible line before any "show more."
4. Draft in the two-line hook format: line 1 is a punchy, complete statement at or under 40 characters; line 2 contrasts or reframes it. The tension between the two lines is what makes someone read line 3.
5. Run the six-angle drill — generate one hook per angle below, all six, before picking. Don't stop at the first one that sounds good.
6. Pick the winning angle by matching it to the campaign's actual goal (awareness, clicks, replies, saves) rather than by personal taste.
7. Check the winning hook against the formula-family notes — specific odd numbers, paid-to-free or free-to-paid reversals, and "X is dead" category-obituary framing have tested strongest; lean toward those shapes when the facts support them honestly.
8. Final pass: strip hedge words, and if this is for X, confirm the hook is not phrased as a question.
9. Once a week (or whenever a batch is due), run the reverse-engineering drill: pull 3 hooks that actually stopped your scroll this week, name which formula family each one is using, then rewrite each for your own topic. This keeps the formula list grounded in what's currently working instead of a fixed set memorized once and never updated.

## two more named patterns

- **Prediction + stakes**: "I think [claim] is the [year] [category] that [consequence]." States a specific, falsifiable prediction and ties a real stake to being wrong or right about it.
- **Tribal-identity split**: "[X] separates [group A] from [group B]." Forces the reader to place themselves on one side of a divide before they've finished reading the line.

Both belong in the same rotation as the six angles above — use them when the topic actually supports a prediction or a real split, not as a reflex.

## the six-angle drill

Generate one hook for each angle, every time, before choosing:

1. **Number-led**: leads with a specific, load-bearing number.
2. **Contrarian**: states the opposite of the conventional wisdom on the topic.
3. **Personal transformation**: before/after framed through one person's experience.
4. **Authority steal**: borrows credibility from a recognizable name, source, or institution, used accurately.
5. **Admission**: opens with a mistake, limitation, or uncomfortable truth about your own side.
6. **Future shock**: states a near-term consequence the reader hasn't priced in yet.

## rules

- Mobile-fold budget: assume roughly 210 characters of attention before a reader decides to bail, tighter on X where the visible line is what matters.
- Two-line hook format: line 1 ≤ 40 characters, a complete punchy statement on its own; line 2 contrasts or reframes line 1.
- Always generate all six angles before selecting. Picking after one draft skips the comparison that finds the strongest option.
- Specific odd numbers ("we cut this to 17 minutes") read as more credible and outperform round numbers ("we cut this to 15 minutes") — use the real number, don't round for cleanliness.
- Paid-to-free and free-to-paid reversals, and "category X is dead / category X is over" framing, are the two formula families that have tested strongest — reach for them first when the underlying fact supports it, and never force one where it isn't true.
- A specific, concrete failure named in the first three lines beats a polished, hedge-free claim — readers trust the writer who admits what didn't work.
- Never hedge in a hook. Cut "I think," "maybe," "sort of," and similar softeners entirely.
- Never open an X hook with a question — it reads as engagement bait and depresses the open rate rather than lifting it.
- Reach for prediction+stakes or tribal-identity split when the topic genuinely supports a forecast or a real divide — don't force either pattern onto a topic that has neither.
