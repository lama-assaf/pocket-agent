# competitive-analysis

competitive analysis exists to find gaps you can occupy, not to feel better about your current position.

## the categories

### direct competitors

same job, same audience. customers consider them an alternative to you.

### adjacent competitors

overlapping job or overlapping audience but not both. customers may not consider them today, but could.

### substitutes

different category that solves the same underlying job (e.g. for a meeting scheduling tool, the substitute is "doing it manually over email").

## per competitor, capture

```
name: [company name]
category: [direct / adjacent / substitute]

positioning
  the one sentence they lead with: [from homepage hero]
  who it speaks to: [target audience]

audience
  who their marketing speaks to (size of company, role, sophistication)
  who their pricing implies they want
  who their case studies feature

pricing
  model: [subscription / usage / freemium / one-time]
  tiers: [list with price points and core inclusions]
  notable: [free tier limits, enterprise gating]

product
  core capabilities: [3-5 bullets]
  notable strengths: [what they do better than others]
  notable gaps: [what they don't do well or at all]
  things only they do: [list]
  things they don't do that customers complain about: [list]

go-to-market
  sales motion: [self-serve / sales-assisted / enterprise sales]
  channel partners: [list]
  notable integrations: [list]

team and traction (only if relevant and discoverable)
  funding stage and amount
  team size and key hires
  publicly known customers

evidence sources: [where each claim above comes from]
```

## positioning map

after the competitor table, build a 2x2 (or 3x3) with axes that matter for the decision the audience is making.

axes that often work:

- price (low / high)
- complexity (simple / powerful)
- audience (smb / enterprise)
- focus (broad platform / specialized tool)

place each competitor on the map. label gaps in the map. those gaps are the positioning opportunities.

## gap analysis

```
gap 1: [specific underserved segment or job]
  evidence: [why we believe this gap exists — customer interviews, support patterns, competitor complaints]
  size: [rough sense of how many customers care]
  defensibility: [why we could win here and not be copied immediately]

gap 2...
```

## what to avoid

- listing features in a checklist as a substitute for positioning analysis
- assuming product detail is the basis of competition (it usually isn't)
- inventing customer concerns about competitors you can't cite
- ranking competitors as winners and losers (it's a map, not a ladder)
- updating once a year (this needs quarterly revisits at minimum)
