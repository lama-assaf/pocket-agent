# launch-planning

a launch is not the day you make it visible. it's the sequence that turns "shipped" into "adopted".

## the inputs

before planning, name these:

1. **who are we trying to reach?** (specific audience, not "everyone who could use it")
2. **what behavior do we want from them?** (sign up, upgrade, try, share, talk about it)
3. **what's the story?** (the one-line and the longer version)
4. **what does success look like?** (specific metrics, specific timeframe)

if any of these are blurry, the launch will be too.

## the launch shape

### pre-launch (week -4 to week -1)

- close beta or early access. find the 10-50 users who'll tell you what's missing.
- prepare assets: landing page, demo, social posts, video if needed, email copy.
- brief internal teams (sales, support, success) so they can answer questions on day 1.
- line up day-one supporters (existing customers willing to vouch, partners willing to amplify).
- prepare for the things that will break (capacity, support volume, integration regressions).

### launch day

- staged rollout if possible. existing customers first, then wider.
- a single moment of release (a blog post, a tweet, a launch on a platform like product hunt).
- the team in one channel ready to respond to questions and issues.

### post-launch (week +1 to week +4)

- daily metric check for the first week
- triage feedback: bugs, confusion, feature requests
- follow-up content (deeper dives, customer stories, case studies)
- re-engage anyone who signed up but didn't activate

## channels worth considering

- direct (existing email list)
- owned (your blog, your social)
- earned (press, podcasts, influential voices in your space)
- communities (where your audience already gathers)
- partner amplification
- paid (only if you know which channel works, otherwise it's expensive learning)

most launches over-index on press and under-index on direct and community.

## the launch narrative

revisit the narrative-architect skill if you're writing the launch story. the short form: status quo → inciting shift → new way → what we built → invitation.

## the day-one risk list

- traffic that breaks something
- a critical bug that surfaces only at scale
- competitor counter-launch
- a customer publicly unhappy with your timing
- a security or privacy issue raised in a comments thread

decide in advance who responds to each and how.

```
launch goal: [one sentence]

audience: [who, specifically]

success metrics:
  primary: [number, timeframe]
  secondary: [list]

narrative:
  short form (1 sentence)
  long form (2-3 paragraphs)

timeline:
  T-4w: [list]
  T-3w: [list]
  T-2w: [list]
  T-1w: [list]
  launch day: [list]
  T+1w: [list]
  T+2w: [list]
  T+4w: [list]

channels:
  owned: [list]
  earned: [list, with target contacts]
  partner: [list]
  paid: [list, with channel and budget]

day-one risks:
  [risk]: [response, owner]

post-launch follow-up plan:
  bug triage: [owner, cadence]
  feedback intake: [owner, mechanism]
  follow-up content: [list, scheduled]
```
