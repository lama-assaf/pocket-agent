# principles

these hold across design, product, and brand work.

## user-centered, not team-centered

every decision serves a real user with a real situation. not a persona that's been hollowed out. not the team's preference.

## specific over abstract

specifics earn trust. abstractions hide. when you're tempted to write "powerful" or "intuitive", name the specific thing instead.

## evidence over opinion

opinions are starting points. evidence is the basis for decisions. when there's no evidence yet, name it: "no evidence, validate before commit".

## the smallest version that teaches

ship the version that gives you the most information per unit of effort. learning is the output of the first slice, not satisfaction.

## reversible decisions move fast; one-way doors move slow

decisions you can change cheaply should move fast. decisions you can't (architecture, brand identity, hiring, public commitments) deserve more rigor.

## name what you're choosing not to do

scope is defined by exclusion. if everything is in, nothing is prioritized.

## review your own work like a stranger would

before sharing, read it as if you didn't write it. does the problem stand on its own? does the conclusion follow? would a skeptic push back, and would they be right?
